Emergency Response Procedures to Incidents for all Outdoor Engine Test-Fires (at Silwood Park Campus)

This document covers a non-exhaustive list of possible incidents which may occur during a test-fire, to which a set of Emergency Response Procedures (ERPs) has been produced. It is expected that all members of the Propulsion Safety Team, and all team personnel are familiar with this document and its contents before any flight can commence.

Incidents and Classification

For the purpose of clarity, “incidents” are defined in this context as any event not considered in the standard test-fire procedure that occurs during the actual execution of the test. In the event of any incident, the severity must be assessed. For all purposes and intents, incidents will be classified and logged according to two key categories:

  1. Minor Incident
    • Any unusual or unexpected flight behavior from the engine under cold operation, which does not result in any harm to personnel whatsoever
    • Any failure of components that does not result in damage to the engine or test bay, and does not result in any harm to personnel whatsoever
  2. Major Incident
    • Any unusual or unexpected engine behavior which results in damage to the engine, pipework, test bay or environment, or causes harm or injury to personnel
    • Any failure of components that cause damage to the engine, pipework, test bay or environment
    • Any event that requires the need to activate the emergency stop function of the test rig
    • Any significant (imminent/potential) danger or harm to persons, infrastructure, and/or property during testing operations
    • Any event that affects persons not from the project

 

Emergency Response Procedures

This section details the necessary, standard steps required to be taken in the event of each incident occurring. Note that the procedures involve the following key personnel, including:

  1. Chief Test Operator
  2. Electronics Technician
  3. Safety Officer

Note that the above is a strict minimum requirement for staff present during engine operation, but is likely to be more expansive for preparatory activities, including representatives from each sub-team, who ensure the correct assembly of their respective subsystems (Storage & Refueling, Throttle Control, Injector/Ignition, Combustion Chamber, and Nozzle).

For the purposes of communication outdoors, airhorns will be supplied (one per group) used to signal different time-critical messages and warnings:

Long Horn

Immediate, impending danger, all personnel to be on high alert for loose flames or shrapnel

Double Horn

Incident has occurred and cannot be rectified, engine is shut off, all personnel to clear take-off area

Multiple Horn (5 beeps)

All clear signal

The above messages are purely for the purpose of warning, and not for any attempt of rectification to be made by personnel beyond the Operations group.

Note that the use of airhorns (opposed to whistles, for instance) is necessitated by the high noise levels observed during test-fires. One will be provided to each subgroup present - those present will be split up in three groups, as prescribed in the Standard Operating Procedure document.

Power-loss failsafe failureNormally-closed valve, designed to cut off the oxidizer feed upon power loss, fails to do so - engine remains operational after loss of power (and so control).

Wait to hear self-induced engine shut-off, then manually turn off the valve.

Electronics Technician

An attempt should be made to restore power - if it cannot be supplied reliably, the test-fire should be called off until this is resolved, and the feed system disconnected.
Engine comes unfastened from test bayFailure of fastening system attaching the engine to the testing rig leads to the engine going loose.The engine should suffer flameout as soon as the feed system disconnects - however, it may travel some distance, spreading fire or causing damage to nearby structures, before coming to a stop. In order to ensure engine shut-off the emergency stop button is pressed.Chief Test OperatorThe test rig and engine are inspected for damage, and the test-fire is called off until the root cause is identified and mitigated.

Premature nozzle closure failureThe nozzle closure, designed to fail under double the expected chamber pressure (60bar), fails prematurely, leading to the nozzle being ejected from the rocket.

Emergency stop button pressed - engine is shut off.

Chief Test OperatorThe test-fire is called off and the part inspected for manufacturing or other flaws.
Sudden change in weather or local conditionsRain, extreme wind, or other inappropriate or inhibitive weather conditions.Emergency stop button pressed - engine is shut off. The test-fire is called off and engine disconnected and moved to the laboratory.Safety OfficerThe engine is inspected, and any weather-related damage mediated.
Nitrous Oxide LeakLeak in the feed system or main valve on the main supply tank causes N2O to be released into the environment (when idle, on assembly or during blowdown test).

Emergency stop button pressed (if operating) - nitrous oxide supply cut off. Immediately disarm ignition system.

If the leak stems from the feed system, it is disconnected and inspected, after safety is ensured, using face shield and insulating gloves.

If the leaks stems from the main supply tank, local authorities are called in, and the area is fully evacuated immediately (PROVISIONAL). Note that all testing takes place in an open-air environment, where ventilation is of little concern.

Safety Officer

If leaks stem from feed system, all relevant items are inspected to retrieve the root cause of failure.

If leaks stem from nitrous oxide tank, the item is safely stored until supplier is contacted for inspection of damage.

Nitrous Oxide tank tip-over

Tip-over of the N2O tank during handling or transportation.

The tank is allowed to come to a stop, if still in motion, and the safety of nearby members is ensured before operations continue.Safety OfficerTank inspected for damage - if green-lit, handling/transportation continues.

Nitrous Oxide exposureVarious means of exposure to nitrous oxide

Eye contact - eye rinsed with water and flush for 15 minutes.

Inhalation - exposed person moved to fresh air, and kept warm and rested. If nausea is caused, immediate assistance should be sought.

Skin contact - thaw frosted parts with lukewarm water.
